\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Every working system that stores, transmits, or processes information operates
ultimately on what its designers call ``data.'' The term appears in database
textbooks, programming language specifications, legal frameworks, and
information-science curricula. Yet it resists a precise, operational definition.
Database texts describe data as ``raw facts'' awaiting
processing~\autocite{rob2013}; information-science references describe data as
facts organized for communication~\autocite{ratzan2004}; international
guidelines call data ``facts, concepts, or instructions expressed in a
standardized way, suitable for being communicated, interpreted, or processed,
whether by people or by automated systems''~\autocite{unesco2008}; and general
dictionaries treat data as ``facts or things known, used as the basis for
inference or calculation''~\autocite{oed2023}. These characterizations are not
wrong, but they are not formal: none specifies what structure a collection of
facts must have before it can be indexed, copied, compared for equality, or
serialized without ambiguity.

This gap is not an academic inconvenience. It surfaces in production systems as
a recognizable family of defects. Schema drift occurs when two components
silently disagree about what an index or field name denotes, despite operating
on byte-identical data. Type confusion~\autocite{cwe843} occurs when a fixed
byte sequence is reinterpreted under a different type assumption than the one
under which it was written. Unsound equality occurs when two values are treated
as equal because they share a representation, despite differing in the value set
the comparison silently assumes. Serialization ambiguity occurs when a wire
format under-specifies the index set or value set well enough that two
conformant implementations disagree on interpretation. Each defect has a common
root: an implicit assumption about the domain, codomain, or mapping underlying
a piece of data was never stated and therefore could not be checked.

Addressing this gap requires more than choosing one informal definition over
another. It requires examining, in sequence, what data are, how data become
meaningful, and how meaning is encoded back into data that machines can
process---that is, it requires a unified account of data, representation, and
interpretation. That is the aim of this paper.

\subsection{Contribution}

This paper contributes three things:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{A lexical and etymological grounding} (Section~\ref{sec:what-is-data})
        that traces the concept of data from its Latin root through
        contemporary usage to expose the structural implication that informal
        definitions share but never state.
  \item \textbf{A formal treatment} (Section~\ref{sec:formal-definition}) of
        data as a total function from an index set to a value set, with
        explicit well-definedness conditions, a precise equality criterion, and
        a systematic account of how common data structures---sequences, tuples,
        multisets---emerge as special cases of this definition.
  \item \textbf{A unified account of data, information, and representation}
        (Sections~\ref{sec:data-information-knowledge}
        and~\ref{sec:data-and-representation}) showing how the formal
        definition grounds the data--information--knowledge hierarchy and how
        the contingency of representation follows directly from the
        function-theoretic view.
\end{enumerate}

We derive four engineering corollaries in Section~\ref{sec:implications},
connecting specific violations of the formal conditions to the failure modes
listed above, and conclude in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} with directions for
future work.

\subsection{Scope and Assumptions}

This paper is concerned with data as treated by computing systems: stored,
addressed, transmitted, and interpreted. It does not attempt a complete
philosophical account of the data--information--knowledge distinction, nor does
it adjudicate the debate over whether data are theory-laden or ``given'' in a
stronger epistemological sense. The formal treatment is intentionally
minimalist: it introduces only the mathematical structure necessary to make the
engineering corollaries precise, and no more. For readers seeking a full
philosophical treatment, Floridi~\autocite{floridi2010} and
Zins~\autocite{zins2007} are the standard entry points.
