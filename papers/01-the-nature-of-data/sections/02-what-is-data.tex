\section{What Is Data?}
\label{sec:what-is-data}

A simple example is the best place to start. Looking at the cross-section of a
tree trunk reveals facts without anyone first explaining what a tree is. The
number of growth rings gives a reasonable estimate of the tree's age; each ring
corresponds to one year of growth, laid down during the annual cycle of dormancy
and activity. The texture and color of the bark suggest the species; warped
grain, discoloration, or insect tunnels indicate health. Look more closely, and
the density of the wood, the direction of the grain, and the moisture content
account for the piece's weight and volume. None of these facts were created the
moment they were noticed; they existed in the tree, available to anyone who knew
how to read them. The correspondence between a physical state and a fact about
the world that can be read off from it is the simplest possible illustration of
what this paper means by \emph{data}.

\subsection{Etymology and Lexical History}

The word \emph{data} is the plural of the Latin \emph{datum}, the past
participle of \emph{dare}, ``to give.'' A datum is, literally, a ``given'': a
piece of information taken as a starting point for reasoning rather than derived
from something else. In everyday speech, the plural form is often treated as a
singular collective noun (``the data is clear''), but in technical writing the
plural is preferred: data \emph{are} observations, measurements, or recorded
facts, not a single undivided thing.

The Latin origin carries real structural weight that survives into technical
usage: a datum is something received from the world, not invented by the observer
who records it. Standard references preserve this sense without formalizing it.

\subsection{Existing Definitions and Their Shared Structure}

Different sources define the term in different, only partially overlapping,
ways. Rob, Morris, and Coronel describe data as \emph{raw facts}: facts that
have not yet been processed for their meaning to become
apparent~\autocite{rob2013}. Ratzan defines the companion term instead:
\emph{information} is a coherent collection of data, organized in a particular
way and meaningful as a result~\autocite{ratzan2004}. The two definitions agree
on more than they disagree about: in both, data precede meaning rather than
supply it directly.

General dictionaries describe much the same shape from a different angle.
Webster's \textit{New International Dictionary} treats data as something given or
accepted: facts or principles granted, the basis from which an inference or a
line of reasoning proceeds, or the material out of which an idealized system is
built~\autocite{websters1934}. The \textit{Oxford English Dictionary} is more
compact: data are facts or things known, used as the basis for inference or
calculation~\autocite{oed2023}. The UNESCO definition adds a structural
requirement absent from the others:

\begin{quote}
Facts, concepts, or instructions expressed in a standardized way, suitable for
being communicated, interpreted, or processed, whether by people or by automated
systems.~\autocite{unesco2008}
\end{quote}

``Expressed in a standardized way'' is the key phrase. It gestures, without
specifying, at the requirement that data have a \emph{structure} that makes
indexing, comparison, and transmission possible. The informal definitions agree
that data precede meaning, but they leave tacit what structural properties make
data addressable.

\subsection{The Structural Implication}

Taken together, these definitions point at something none of them states
outright. Data have no shape that belongs to them independently of some
particular point of view. A fact only counts as data if it sits within a
framework---of meaning, of purpose, or of use---relative to which it is relevant,
organized, coherent, and useful. A growth ring means nothing to a system with no
concept of seasons; a voltage means nothing to a circuit built to ignore it.
Data presuppose some prior agreement, however informal, about what is worth
attending to in the first place.

This structural implication is what the formal definition in
Section~\ref{sec:formal-definition} makes precise. Before arriving there, two
properties of data that are easily overlooked deserve explicit attention.

\subsection{The Context-Dependence of Data}

The same physical signal can constitute different data depending on the framework
applied to it. Consider five bytes with hexadecimal values
\texttt{0x41 0x52 0x4C 0x49 0x5A}. Under one set of assumptions, this is the
ASCII string \texttt{"ARLIZ"}; under another, it is five unsigned 8-bit integers:
65, 82, 76, 73, and 90; under a third, it is the first five bytes of an
arbitrary binary file format; under a fourth, a fragment of a network packet
payload. The bytes themselves are identical in every case. What changes is not
the underlying signal, but the interpretation imposed by context. Meaning is not
contained in the bytes; it arises from the agreement between the system that
produced them and the system that reads them.

This example also illustrates the practical consequence of ignoring
context-dependence: if the framework is not stated, different components will
apply different frameworks to the same bytes, producing different and
incompatible results. The defects described in Section~\ref{sec:introduction}
are exactly this phenomenon at scale.

\subsection{The Perishability of Data}

A common assumption early in one's technical career is that data are fixed and
objective: you record a fact, and the fact is stored. This is only partially
true, and the part that is not true matters. Walliman observes that data are not
only imprecise but \emph{transient} and \emph{perishable}~\autocite{walliman2011}.
A poll measuring voting intentions ahead of an election will not produce the same
result as an otherwise identical poll conducted at a different time, even with
the same sampling method and, in principle, the same respondents.

``Perishable'' does not mean that data physically vanish. A measurement recorded
yesterday is still there to be read today. What perishes is its
\emph{currency}: its claim to describe the present state of whatever it measured.
Data perish in a second sense as well. Secondhand reports and biased accounts are
routinely presented as settled fact, because no method of recording information
is perfectly reliable, and some distortion enters at almost every stage of
transmission. This is not a rare edge case confined to opinion polling; it is a
fundamental property of how facts move from the world into a recorded form. The
implication for engineering is that reliable data require explicit attention to
precision, integrity, provenance, and timestamp---properties that follow
naturally from the formal definition in Section~\ref{sec:formal-definition}.
