\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Every working system that stores, transmits, or processes information
ultimately operates on what its designers call ``data.'' Yet the term resists
a precise, operational definition. Database textbooks describe data as
``raw facts'' that have not yet been processed into meaning~\autocite{Rob2013};
information-science references describe data as a coherent organization of
such facts~\autocite{Ratzan2004}; international guidelines describe data as facts
or instructions ``expressed in a standardized way'' suitable for communication
or processing~\autocite{UNESCO2008}; and general dictionaries treat data as
``facts or things known, used as the basis for inference''~\autocite{Webster1934}.
These characterizations are not wrong, but they are not formal. None specifies
what structure a collection of facts must have before it can be indexed,
copied, compared for equality, or serialized.

This gap surfaces in production systems as a recognizable family of defects:

\begin{description}
  \item[Schema drift.]
    Two components silently disagree about what an index or field name
    denotes, despite operating on byte-identical data.
  \item[Type confusion.]
    A fixed byte sequence is reinterpreted under a different type assumption
    than the one under which it was written---the structural basis of an
    entire class of memory-safety and deserialization vulnerabilities~\autocite{CWE843}.
  \item[Unsound equality.]
    Two values are treated as equal because they share a representation,
    despite differing in the value set the comparison silently assumes.
  \item[Serialization ambiguity.]
    A wire format under-specifies the index set or value set well enough
    that two conformant implementations disagree on interpretation.
\end{description}

Each defect has a common root: an implicit assumption about the
\emph{domain}, \emph{codomain}, or \emph{mapping} underlying a piece of data
was never stated, and therefore could not be checked.

\subsection{Contribution}

This paper contributes:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A formal statement of the function-theoretic definition with explicit
        well-definedness conditions (Section~\ref{sec:definition}), clarifying
        its relationship to sequences, tuples, multisets, and relations.
  \item A comparative analysis (Section~\ref{sec:related}) positioning the
        definition against Floridi's GDI, Zins' faceted classification, and
        the DIKW hierarchy.
  \item Four corollaries (Section~\ref{sec:implications}) connecting specific
        structural properties to recurring engineering failure modes, with
        worked examples.
  \item A discussion (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}) of scope, limitations,
        and directions for future work.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Scope}

This paper is concerned with data as treated by computing systems: stored,
addressed, transmitted, and interpreted. It does not attempt a complete
philosophical account of the data--information--knowledge distinction, nor
does it adjudicate the broader debate over whether data are theory-laden or
``given'' in some stronger epistemological sense~\autocite{Floridi2005}.
