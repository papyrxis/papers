\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Informal and Lexical Definitions}

The etymological root of \emph{data}---the Latin \emph{datum},
``that which is given''---already carries a structural implication:
a datum is received rather than derived, a starting point for inference
rather than its product~\autocite{Webster1934}. Standard reference works
preserve this sense without formalizing it. Rob and Coronel define data as
raw, unprocessed facts~\autocite{Rob2013}; Ratzan characterizes
\emph{information} as a coherent, organized collection of
data~\autocite{Ratzan2004}; UNESCO adds a requirement that data be
``expressed in a standardized way, suitable for being communicated,
interpreted, or processed''~\autocite{UNESCO2008}---the closest any of these
sources comes to a structural requirement.

\subsection{Floridi's General Definition of Information}

Floridi's General Definition of Information (GDI) characterizes information
as data that are well-formed, meaningful, and (in some variants) truthful,
with data themselves treated as ``lack of uniformity'' between two states of
a system~\autocite{Floridi2010}. The GDI deliberately treats the underlying
notion of data as a primitive; the function-theoretic definition supplies the
structural detail that the GDI leaves unspecified at the data level by design.

\subsection{Zins' Faceted Classification}

Zins surveys a wide range of conceptions of data, information, and knowledge
and organizes them into faceted categories, distinguishing views that treat
data as \emph{objects} from views that treat data as \emph{signals} or
\emph{representations} of facts~\autocite{Zins2007}. The function-theoretic
definition proposes a structure compatible with the \emph{representation}
facet specifically, on the grounds that representation---something standing
for something else, addressably---is the facet computing systems actually
instantiate.

\subsection{The DIKW Hierarchy}

The data--information--knowledge--wisdom hierarchy~\autocite{Ackoff1989,Rowley2007}
is widely used to describe the progressive enrichment of data through
organization and interpretation. It is frequently presented without a
precise definition of its lowest tier. The function-theoretic definition is
intended as a precise characterization of exactly that tier, compatible with
and presupposed by every DIKW-style account.
